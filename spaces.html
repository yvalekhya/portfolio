Spaces Design Project





Overview

Several students have expressed frustration about the lack of preferable study spots on campus; however, a pragmatic solution to this specific pain point remains to be accomplished.




What is "Spaces"?

Spaces is an interactive interface that assists students in finding the available study spots on campus, emerging from the common challenges faced by students, especially concerning the Shield’s library.

On top of studying for the difficult exams during midterms and finals seasons, students have voiced their struggles to actually find spaces on campus to study. Our group sent out a survey to UC Davis students to further investigate the relationship between students’ study habits and being able to find study spaces on campus. Based on the responses gathered, 75% of the students expressed that it was quite difficult to find spots to study on campus, as it would take them 20-30 minutes to look for them. The unfortunate truth, however, is that even after searching, students don’t always find study spots. When students are unable to find study spots, it affects their overall productivity and motivation to study in addition to increasing their frustration and stress in an already stressful time. Students tend to (1) go home to study, which ends up in getting distracted and losing focus, (2) postpone studying, which ends up in cramming right before the exam, or (3) alter sleep schedules, which ends up in them pulling all-nighters.

Conceptual Model

After setting the problem scope and problem statement, our group focused the attention to crafting a list of possible recommendations and solutions. For our concept to be pragmatic, we also debated the most acceptable forms of interfaces on which we would possibly execute it. The conceptual layout circles around a schematic seat map indicating occupied and vacant seats with distinct colors. One of the many ways it solves the problem is by assisting students, who earlier would go up and down the individual floors to find seating of their choice, can now see before-hand which parts of the study spaces are available. This optimizes the process from a brute-force approach. Based on the survey, students also mentioned they prefer some seats over others based on their needs such as better lighting, better wi-fi connectivity, proximity to wall sockets etc. We included filters for the seat map, indicating ideal seats based on their preferences. The execution is based on our desire to make the entire process efficient. We followed HCI principles such as visibility and accessibility in order to make students understand and utilize our product’s features and functionalities. To tackle accessibility, we included our concept as an extension of the UC Davis app and plan to have physical structures containing our concept at different parts of the library to make it more visible.





Prototype / Implementation

After conceptualizing, we chose to make our concept a website view due to the constraints we have faced regarding the Library’s vast floor plan. It was difficult to condense the floor plan into a mobile view considering all the filters, seat views, and table views for each and every floor. We started off our process with creating a myUC Davis tile for our concept “Study Spaces.” From the data we collected through our interviews and surveys, it was apparent that the majority of the students study at the Library on campus so we chose to focus on the Shields Library for our concept. We included a floor plan overview of the five floors of the library as an introductory page. We chose to make it aesthetically pleasing to attract an element of visual design. After clicking on a specific floor, we created a floor view showing the different sections. We included filters and preference selections on the right hand side of the section view, and a color coded key showing the overview of how many seats there are available on the left hand side of the section view page. We included screens of all edge cases such as “no spaces available” for too many preferences selected.

We included the table view and seat view separately in case people wanted to view the floor plans of each, and so the design would look seamless and clean. We wanted to display the information in a clean, minimalistic design so the information would not look too cluttered or frustrating for the user. We chose to do a table view first due to the variety of table layouts for each section in the library. We chose to have gray represent “no available spaces” and green to represent “available spaces” for the table view. We included a pop-up screen for the seat view that users can click on, for each green table view. For the seat view, we chose to label the unavailable seats in red, and the available seats in green. Due to the time constraints, our prototype is limited in functionality since we were not able to consider all 5 floors of the library, including the different sections, and table and seat layouts.





User Testing

Our group decided to conduct user testing to see whether UC Davis students understood the interactions with the Spaces web application as well as get their feedback on a color palette that made the sections in the floorplan distinguishable. A total of 10 UC Davis students took part in the user testing sessions. For the first part of user testing, our group presented UC Davis students with scenarios for them to walk through. As an example of a scenario we presented to users, they were asked to find a seat on the first floor of the library with specific filters selected. The goal was to see if users could easily go through the task, without confusion or difficulty. If our users could understand the potential scenarios students may go through when interacting with application and easily use our product, we were on the right track. The second part of user testing consisted of presenting a few color palette variations and asking users to select their preferred one. Our aim for this was to narrow down a color palette that made each of the sections of the floorplan distinguishable but also cohesive.

Following the user testing sessions, our group reviewed over and analyzed the results. For the first portion of testing, users were able to easily understand and complete the different scenarios. This was the validation our group needed to move forward with our app flow and refine our design. For the second portion of testing, after presenting various color palette options, the majority of the users preferred the 3rd option we presented. Their reasoning for this was that the color scheme was overall very cohesive and the sections were easily identifiable. They did, however, provide us with suggestions to fix the color for one section in particular, as it stood out a bit from the rest. With the recommendations posed by our users, we proceeded to refine the color palette and finalized our design.





Discussion

The aim for the “Spaces” concept was to come up with a solution that was mostly visual and highly interactive for users. Our solution, hence, was based on having floor plans, table views, and seat maps. However, we did run into a few challenges when designing, particularly with representing the library with full accuracy. When our group visited the library to get an idea how the floors were represented, the table and seat views, we found it hard to completely mimic every floor in the library. For example, some floors have a corresponding ½ floor and the central part of every floor has a pillar, which would hamper our overall layout. Each of the floorplans for the 5 floors of the library are also different, which made it more difficult to represent all the floorplans of the library. Having that said, our design does take into account all the 5 floors in the library and mimics the seat layout to a plausible scenario. We chose to focus on one floor specifically, the 1st floor of Shields Library, to incorporate all the functionality that we planned for, including filters and preferences, sections, and table and seat views.

This project really emphasized the iterative nature of design, from the initial stages of defining the problem statement and understanding our users’ needs to sketching and prototyping a solution that would address our core issue. In particular, the most challenging part of our project was not actually the logistical issues or lack of resources and time; it was defining the problem statement and scoping out our project. Being able to figure out what the actual problem was for our users and conceptualizing a solution that addressed this problem was our main challenge. However, after numerous iterations of drafting the problem statement and requirements for the solution, designing the solution was not so difficult, and we did not have to make lots of changes to our concept after user testing. The medium-fidelity prototype that we designed still has a lot of room for improvement, but we were able to create a solution that matched our vision and addressed the core problem.





Future Work

In the future, we would like to include all the floors of the library and have more accurate seat mapping. Students, who earlier would go up and down the individual floors to find seating of their choice, would be able to see before-hand which parts of the study spaces are available, thus improving the process from a brute-force approach. Currently, our prototype does not work with all the filters, so we would need to add additional functionality. The filters will make the process of finding an efficient spot easier. Another aspect of this problem we noticed from the survey responses was the difficulty faced by students in group studies. Till now, our prototype just shows the availability of the seats on the first floor, however, Spaces is more than that. We plan to tackle this problem by also including a section of the library available for reservation in our future implementation. We want it to let the users reserve their desired spots so that they can be ensured that the vacant spot is not taken by anyone else. To determine vacancy and occupancy of seats, we would also use weight sensors for individual seats. Lastly, for this project, we would like to expand Spaces by including more study areas on campus like the Silo, Memorial Union, and Student Community Center.
